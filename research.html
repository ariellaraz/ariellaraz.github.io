Current Work

Mathematical modelling of cytoskeletal signalling circuits controlling lamellipodial protrusion and persistent cell migration

My research develops data-informed dynamical systems models to understand how interactions within the actin cytoskeleton generate organised protrusion dynamics at the leading edge of migrating cells.

Biological Context

Cell migration plays an important role in wound healing, immune response, and cancer metastasis. Migrating cells extend protrusions at their leading edge called lamellipodia, where actin polymerisation drives membrane motion.

Experimental work suggests that regulators such as Lamellipodin (Lpd), NHSL1, ARPIN, and Rac signalling form interacting feedback and feedforward circuits that control protrusion dynamics and migration persistence.

Modelling Objective

The central modelling question is:

Which minimal interaction structures are sufficient to organise protrusion dynamics and persistent migration?

The aim is to identify reduced dynamical systems that capture the key behaviours observed experimentally while avoiding unnecessary biochemical complexity.

Modelling Framework

The modelling strategy proceeds in stages:

Data interpretation: analyse time series derived from live-cell imaging experiments measuring protein activity and membrane motion.
Circuit reconstruction: identify candidate feedback and feedforward interaction structures within cytoskeletal signalling networks.
Dynamical systems modelling: construct minimal ODE models describing the temporal dynamics of the signalling circuits.
Spatial extension: extend the models to spatial reaction-diffusion PDEs defined along the cell edge.
Analysis: investigate stability, oscillatory behaviour, and spatial coordination within the inferred models.

Nonlinear Mechanisms

Key nonlinear processes incorporated in the modelling include retrograde actin flow, force-velocity coupling between actin polymerisation and membrane motion, and positive and negative feedback within signalling circuits.

These mechanisms are hypothesised to generate oscillatory protrusion cycles and regulate migration persistence.

Computational Methods

The models are simulated using numerical methods for dynamical systems and partial differential equations.

Computational tools include Python-based simulation workflows, finite element discretisation where spatial modelling is required, and FEM frameworks such as Firedrake.

Collaboration

This project is carried out in close collaboration with an experimental cytoskeleton research group. High-resolution imaging experiments provide time-series measurements of protein activity and membrane dynamics.

Model development and experimental analysis proceed iteratively, with simulations used to interpret experimental observations and guide further experiments.

Long-Term Goal

The broader aim is to develop mechanistic mathematical models that clarify how cytoskeletal signalling circuits regulate cell migration and how these mechanisms may change in metastasising cancer cells.
