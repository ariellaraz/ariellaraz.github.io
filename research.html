Research aim
Identify minimal dynamical mechanisms within cytoskeletal circuits that regulate polarised lamellipodial protrusion, persistence, and directed migration, and how these are altered in cancer cells.

Quantitative measurements from collaborator-led live-cell imaging experiments tracking cytoskeletal protein activity and membrane dynamics at the leading edge of migrating cells.
Model
Parameterised dynamical systems models of cytoskeletal regulation, ranging from reduced ODE systems to reaction-diffusion PDEs on evolving cell surfaces coupled to membrane dynamics.
Nonlinearity
Nonlinear feedback between cytoskeletal signalling, actin polymerisation, and membrane mechanics.
Computation
Numerical simulation, parameter estimation, and analysis of PDE models using scientific computing methods, including evolving surface finite element techniques.

PhD Research

Current Work

Systems-Level Modelling of Cytoskeletal Networks Controlling Cancer Cell Migration

My research focuses on the mathematical modelling component of the wider CytoNet project, developing systems-level models to understand how cytoskeletal networks regulate cancer cell migration.

The modelling approach focuses on reduced dynamical systems that capture the essential regulatory mechanisms underlying polarised protrusion, persistence, and migration while avoiding unnecessary biochemical complexity.

Biological Context

Cell migration plays an important role in wound healing, immune response, and cancer metastasis.

Migrating cells extend protrusions at their leading edge known as lamellipodia, where actin polymerisation drives membrane advancement.

A central question in the project is how persistence is regulated during directed migration in non-transformed cells and how this regulation is altered in cancer cells.

Modelling Objective

The central modelling question is:

Which minimal dynamical interaction structures are sufficient to organise cytoskeletal activity, sustained protrusion, and directed cell migration?

The aim is to construct reduced mathematical models that capture essential features of cytoskeletal regulation while remaining focused on the mathematics of the core regulatory circuitry.

Research Approach

The modelling strategy combines biological motivation, published literature, and dynamical systems theory to study the cytoskeletal circuits underlying protrusion, persistence, and directed migration.

Data interpretation
Quantitative observations and published experimental results are used to inform and constrain candidate dynamical models of cytoskeletal activity and protrusion dynamics.

Model construction
Minimal interaction networks are proposed to represent key regulatory relationships within cytoskeletal signalling.

Dynamical systems modelling
Reduced ODE systems are used to investigate the temporal behaviour of signalling circuits, including feedback loops capable of generating oscillations, persistence, and polarity.

Spatial modelling
Models are extended to spatial reaction-diffusion PDE systems on evolving cell surfaces, coupled to membrane mechanics and, where required, retrograde flow and external chemotactic or haptotactic cues.

Analysis
Stability, oscillatory behaviour, pattern formation, persistence, and spatial coordination within the models are investigated using analytical and computational methods.

Biological settings
The models are designed to study random migration, chemotaxis, and haptotaxis, and to help explain how migration control differs between non-transformed cells and metastasising cancer cells.

Collaboration

This work forms the mathematical modelling strand of a broader interdisciplinary project. My main supervisor is Chandrasekhar Venkataraman at the University of Sussex, and the project is carried out in collaboration with an experimental cytoskeleton research group at King’s College London.

Model development and experimental work proceed iteratively, with simulations used to interpret observations, explore candidate mechanisms, and help guide further investigation.

Long-Term Goal

The long-term aim of this research is to develop mechanistic mathematical models that clarify how cytoskeletal networks regulate migration behaviour and how that regulation changes in cancer cells. By identifying the dynamical mechanisms that organise protrusion and migration persistence, these models aim to provide a theoretical framework for understanding how migration behaviour arises in biological systems and how it may be altered in metastatic disease.
